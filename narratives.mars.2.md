Runnerbats had taken an infant in Jarthax.  They had opened the front
door of a house on the edge of town in the middle of the night,
stalked in, and took only the one child.  The red dirt street was
tracked with the spoor of them.  Yokanân set his eyeglasses down on
his desk before him where he sat, pinched the bridge of his nose with
thumb and forefinger and sighed.  

He made the Sign of the Cross, then looked for a moment to be in
silent prayer.  "Has the child been found, or the child's body, by
some miracle perhaps?"

"No, Father.  But you know that," the Reeve told him gently.  One
would think the man had a habit of taking care of the good Father,
which was in fact the truth.

"Yes I suppose I do," he told the Reeve.  Father Yokanân had a harried
look, as though his world were a little more than he could manage.  In
fact it *was*, he knew, more than he could manage, but such is true of
every sapient the whole Galaxy round.  He told himself this quite
often.  He had a quick mental image of the hideous mammals sprinting
into the darkness of the deep desert on the limbs that used to be
their ancestors' wings, one of them holding a bloodied screaming baby
in the prehensile hands that dangled beneath its body, shrill shrieks
and clicks of echolocation fading into the outer dark.

Jarthax was a small town on the outskirts of the Low-Canals Region.
Yokanân was no bishop. He was only a priest in the city of Trellin,
where there were hundreds of churches each with its own priest. He had
no authority in the case concerning the predation of the baby. What he
did have was experience of the Xeric Wastelands to which the *gîgôken*
had seemingly returned, and the oases hidden in that desert, one of
which would be the runnerbats' nestingplace.  In most respects he was
not a hard man, but his father had been a hunter and something of an
outlaw. His own choices had largely been made in reaction against his
father, but that was after his conversion at the age of sixteen
Martian years. Till then he too had been something of a hunter and a
wolf's head. He might not put a stop to the killings entirely, but he
could prevent a few deaths, of that much he was certain. God knew no
one else probably would even try. And Bishop Miwax had been urging him
to take a sabbatical leave for years. *And we know that in
everything God works for the good of those who love him, who have been
called according to his purpose.*

But if he started something, Yokanân thought, if he showed initiative,
a rare quality in a society of complacent addicts and de facto slaves
who had been taught since birth to love their entirely metaphorical
chains, a possibility remained that he would not have to act alone.
The man might be an addict himself, but he was no complacent slave,
and he was, after all, Father Yokanân's own brother.
